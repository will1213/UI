Heuristic Evaluation

Process

Our evaluators used Nielsen and Molich’s 10 User Interface Design Heuristics to evaluate the four currently prototyped user tasks in our project. As mentioned before, the user tasks we prototyped include: (1) browsing libraries of movies and shows, (2) searching for specific movies and shows, (3) watching movies and shows using an embedded video player, and (4) aggregating and displaying ratings of movies and shows. Each evaluator independently performed the four user tasks through our high-fidelity prototype and recorded the issues they experienced while also including details regarding which usability heuristics were violated. The remaining team members (i.e., reviewers) then reviewed and analyzed the evaluators’ observations, rating each issue on a scale of severity from 0 (non-issue) to 4 (usability catastrophe). After the evaluators and reviewers independently completed their tasks, all members came together to discuss the issues and brainstorm ideas to resolve them.

Findings

After the evaluators completed their heuristic evaluation documents, they presented them to the reviewers to review and analyze. While working separately, each reviewer created a table in which they listed the issues that were observed by the evaluators, as well as the usability heuristics the issues violated and the severity of the violations. Once the reviewers completed their tables, they aggregated their data into one table to more simply showcase the issues found by the evaluators. After this, all members of the team met up to discuss the findings and determine which issues needed to be fixed in this iteration. Through this process, we found 11 issues with our prototype, which violated a usability heuristic from Nielsen and Molich’s 10 User Interface Design Heuristics. For the most part, our four prototyped user tasks were able to achieve their promised functionalities. The reason for this is because we never reached the maximum severity of 4 (usability catastrophe) for any issue. In fact, our average severity for all 11 issues is 2.27. Nonetheless, we were determined to fix a few issues.

First, let us discuss which issues we decided not to fix. Since our prototype has limited and controlled user actions, there is no way to break it in any way. As a result, we decided to leave the issues of not having error messages or help documentation for our next prototype iteration. As well, we decided not to fix issues that could not be fixed through our prototyping software, Figma, due to limitations of what can be prototyped in it. For example, one of our evaluators noted that there is no way to perform single-character deletion in our prototype’s search bar. But the reason for this is because we could not find an easy/built-in way to do this type of prototyping in Figma without using external plugins.

Now, let us discuss which issues we decided to fix. Basically, we decided to fix the rest of our issues which were not mentioned in the previous paragraph. Most of them are just minor oversights and fixing them increases the quality of life of our users. So, it seemed reasonable to focus on these ones first. As for our solutions for fixing these issues, we were inspired heavily, if not completely, by what the evaluators wrote down in the “How can this rule further improve usability, utility, and desirability” column of their heuristic evaluations.
